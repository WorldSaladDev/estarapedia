The history plant is a sprawling broad-leaved vine originally found in mountainous regions, though is often observed spreading across ruins, castle walls, and other remnants of civilization.

Description

The history plant is a perennial vine with a long lifespan. Individual vines can persist for decades under favorable conditions, continually spreading and regenerating. Colonies of the plant often grow interconnected through a shared root network, allowing them to endure for centuries. This longevity, combined with its ability to thrive in human-made structures like ruins, has led to its frequent association with historical sites, inspiring its common name.

Vines

The vines of the history plant are strong but flexible, and take on a slightly woody texture as they mature, allowing them to withstand harsh seasonal changes. Newly-grown portions of the vines are covered in soft hairs that help retain moisture and protect against minor abrasion.

Foliage

The history plant has broad, lush leaves that are dark green with a slightly waxy texture, allowing them to retain moisture in rocky or exposed conditions. The leaves grow in an alternating pattern along its vines.

Pods

The history plant forms large, roughly spherical pods that house a dense concentration of reproductive spores. These pods have a firm, muted green or brown casing and can grow up to 5 centimeters in diameter.

Roots

The roots of the history plant are thin and fibrous, spreading out in an extensive network to cling to rock faces and brick walls. These roots anchor the plant securely, allowing it to cover large areas with its photosynthetic leaves. The fibrous nature of the roots enables them to weave into cracks and crevices, gradually establishing a strong foothold even in harsh, nutrient-poor environments.

Reproduction

When a colony of history plants determines conditions to be favorable, mature pods split open in unison throughout the colony, releasing a thick, colorful haze of spores into the air. This synchronized release ensures maximum spore dispersal, allowing the vine to colonize a wide area quickly.

Distribution and Habitat

The history plant prefers to root itself in alumino-silicate clay (a naturally occuring mineral often used as brick mortar) throughout mountains and cliff faces and in the settlements of northern and southeastern Ekhala. It thrives in dry continental climates, but has adapted to live in less favorable conditions as well.

Uses

Despite its reputation, gardeners and horticulturists in rural areas have cultivated ornamental varieties of the history plant that produce spores in a range of vibrant colors, making them a popular choice for decorative displays. The various colored spores are also widely used in dyemaking, and the spore pods are sometimes sold whole for use as natural smoke bombs for festivals.
